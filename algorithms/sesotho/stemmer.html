Introduction

The Sesotho (Southern Sotho) stemming algorithm was developed by Kamohelo Lebjane. Sesotho (ISO 639-1: st) is a Southern Bantu language spoken by approximately 5.6 million people in South Africa and Lesotho.

Like other Bantu languages, Sesotho is agglutinative - words are formed by attaching prefixes and suffixes to root morphemes. A single root can appear with many different affixes, making stemming important for information retrieval.

Stemming Approach

The algorithm removes noun class prefixes and verb/nominal suffixes to extract root forms. Sesotho uses a noun class system where prefixes indicate grammatical categories (e.g., mo-/ba- for people, le-/ma- for certain objects, se-/li- for things). Verb suffixes encode tense, aspect, and voice (e.g., -ile for perfect tense, -isa for causative, -wa for passive).

The stemmer processes suffixes before prefixes and ensures a minimum stem length is preserved to avoid over-stemming.
